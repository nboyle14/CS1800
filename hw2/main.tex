\item Let $k$ be an integer. \\
Claim: If $5k+3$ is even, then $k$ is odd. \\
Prove the claim by first converting to contrapositive. \\   % Don't use contradiction at any point.  \\
%{\em For practice (without submitting), think about how to prove this directly, and also by contradiction.}\\

{\color{blue}
$Proof:$\\
By contrapositive, assume $k$ is an even integer\\
$k=2n$ where n is an integer\\
$5k+3=5(2n)+3\\
=10n+3$\\
$=10n+2+1\\
=2(5n+1)+1$\\
$=(2j)+1$ where $j$ is an integer\\
$=5k+3=2j+1$ which is odd by definition, thus proving $k$ is odd by contrapositive\\
$\blacksquare$
}

\item  Suppose that $x$ and $y$ are positive real numbers.
Prove by contradiction that (for all such $x,y$) $x+y \geqslant 2\sqrt{xy}$\\

{\color{blue}
$Proof:$\\
by contradiction, assume $x+y< 2\sqrt{xy}$\\
$x^{2}+2xy+y^{2}<4xy$\\
$x^{2}-2xy+y^{2}<0$\\
$(x-y)^{2}<0$\\
This is a contradiction since the square of a real number is always non-negative.\\
$\blacksquare$
}

\item Prove the following, by smallest counterexample. 

For all $n\geqslant 1$, $\sum\limits_{k=1}^n k(k{+}1) = \frac{n(n{+}1)(n{+}2)}{3}$


{\color{blue}
$Proof:$\\\\
True Statement: $2+6+12+...+n(n-1) = \frac{n(n-1)(n+1)}{3}$\\\\
Smallest Counterexample: $2+6+12+...+n(n-1)+n(n+1) \neq \frac{n(n+1)(n+2)}{3}$\\\\
$\frac{n(n-1)(n+1)}{3}+n(n+1) \neq \frac{n(n+1)(n+2)}{3}$\\\\
$n(n-1)(n+1) + 3n(n+1) \neq n(n+1)(n+2)$\\\\
$n(n+1) \cdot (n-1+3) \neq n(n+1)(n+2)$\\\\
$n(n+1) \cdot (n+2) \neq n(n+1)(n+2)$\\\\
$n(n+1)(n+2) \neq n(n+1)(n+2)$\\\\
This is a contradiction. Thus, there is no smallest counter example and the claim is true.\\\\
$\blacksquare$
}
